My Coding Journey so Far (Part 1)

Learn to Code, Front end, Web development, Freecodecamp

Jan 24, 2024 | 4 Min Read

It has been a little over a month since I resumed my efforts to learn to code, I started around December 30, and I have been coding ever since. I am here to share everything I’ve learned so far.

Timeline of What and How I Have Learned

At first, I started as most people do, watching videos on YouTube. Some of the tutorials that I watched were from creators like Tech with Tim, Javascript Mastery, and Fireship, But I didn’t learn much except the very basics of Python and JavaScript. Therefore I wanted a more structured way to learn, and I found a free resource called Freecodecamp i spent almost an entire month just doing their responsive web design course. It was overall pretty great, and it was very easy to get started compared to watching YouTube videos where you would have to set up the development environment yourself. About 1/4 through the courses I was getting a little bored so I tried Codecademy I got a free trial of their pro membership and I took a course on command line basics, which was very random but has been helping me set up my development environment for the Odin Project. After that, I was back at Freecodecamp and on January 20 I finished the course. For the past few days, I have started 2 new courses, CS50x and the Odin Project. I’m not sure which one I like more and will commit to, but I think it is the Odin Project because the long lectures in CS50x are just so long, compared to the fairly quick reading that you do when taking the Odin Project.

Coding Resources

In this section, I will rate each one of the coding resources I have used so far on a scale of 1–10.

Youtube

YouTube was the first way I and many other people started to learn to code. Overall youtube is mostly bad because of the risk of you falling into TUTORIAL HELL. If you haven’t heard of tutorial hell it is a state where you constantly watch tutorials, but you don’t apply what you have learned, this causes you to think you know a lot more than you do, and it causes you to barely remember anything. Other than that the tutorials I watched on YouTube were ok they were not anything great. So overall I would rate YouTube a 4.5/10.

Freecodecamp

Freecodecamp was very good, It is the most beginner-friendly course out of any of these, and requires almost no setup and it is 100% free. The courses are pretty easy but they do get harder. It also makes you create your projects which helps you apply your learning, and build your portfolio. However I did recently take their Javascript course, but I did not enjoy it as it is still in Beta and has bugs. However, this may change in the future. Overall I would rate Freecodecamp an 8/10.

Codecademy

Codecademy was pretty good, I barely did it so I do not have much experience with it however I do like the way the courses are structured, but I do not like how so many are paid. The best thing about Codecademy is the wide range of courses it has, it is best if you want to learn something more niche because it just has so many courses. The courses themselves aren’t amazing but they aren’t bad either. I just feel that 30–40$ a month is overpriced and you can find great tutorials on YouTube if you are learning something niche. Overall I would rate Codecademy a 6/10.

CS50x

CS50x is the course I have the least experience with. However, I can still see the downfalls and upsides to it. CS50 or CS50x is great for complete beginners to programming. However, it does require more math knowledge and more general knowledge as the course is given at Harvard. The long lectures can get boring if you do not have a very long attention span, so this made me constantly want to take breaks. However, there are so many people to ask for and the community is just great. So overall I would rate CS50x a 7/10.

The Odin Project

The Odin Project is a free and open-source course on full-stack web development. It does not have many options regarding what coding language you want to learn (either Ruby with Rails or Javascript with Node) But I would say this is a good thing as it prevents you from learning a bunch of different programming languages that you do not need to know. It also has a great community, however, it may be a little harder for beginners as it moves a bit faster than other courses and expects you to do a little of your research. Overall I would rate The Odin Project 8.5/10.
